## Brainstorm

The Brainstorm slide runs your audience through three sequential stages on a single slide: submitting ideas, voting on their favourites, and viewing results ranked by vote count. Participants submit ideas as free text, then you advance to the voting stage where everyone can vote for the ideas they like most (you control how many votes each person gets). Finally, you reveal results with the most-voted ideas ranked at the top. You can show or hide submissions and vote counts at each stage for a more dramatic reveal. Each slide supports up to 200 submitted ideas.

**When to use it:** Group ideation exercises, retrospective action items, team decision-making, or any situation where you want to collect ideas and let the crowd surface the best ones.

<Note>
  In self-paced mode, participants who complete the slide early won't see ideas submitted after they voted. For best results in self-paced sessions, ask everyone to submit first, then open voting after all submissions are in.
</Note>

## Rating Scale

The Rating Scale slide lets you pose a broad question, add up to 8 specific statements beneath it, and ask your audience to rate each statement on a numeric scale. The default is a 1–5 scale, but you can extend it to any range below 1,000 for more refined responses. You label the low and high ends of the scale (for example, *"Very dissatisfied"* to *"Very satisfied"*) and optionally label every value in between — these labels appear on participants' devices to guide their answers. Results display as a colour-coded graph with an average score for each statement.

**When to use it:** Post-event satisfaction surveys, training feedback with multiple dimensions, employee pulse checks, or any time you need nuanced numeric responses across several criteria at once.

## Ranking

The Ranking slide asks participants to prioritise a list of up to 10 options, not just pick one. Participants tap to select the items they want to rank, drag them into their preferred order, and submit. Results use a Borda count weighted scoring system: the item ranked first by a participant gets the most points, and so on down the list. The item with the highest total points across all participants wins — meaning the top result reflects collective priority, not just the most frequently chosen item.

**When to use it:** Workshop prioritisation, strategy sessions, feature backlog ordering, retrospective action planning, or any scenario where you need the group to agree on *what matters most* rather than just what they like.

## Categorise {#categorise}

The Categorise slide is a scored quiz type where participants drag shuffled items into the correct category buckets on their phones while results appear live on your presenter screen. You define up to 8 categories and assign items to them in the editor. Participants see all items shuffled and must sort each one into the right bucket before the timer runs out. Supports partial scoring (points for each correctly sorted item) and the standard quiz scoring settings — max and min points, faster answers get more points, and a timer.

**When to use it:** Knowledge checks on classification, corporate training on categories or tiers, classroom sorting exercises, or any quiz question where the answer involves *grouping* rather than *selecting*.

## Match Pairs {#match-pairs}

The Match Pairs slide is a scored quiz type where participants connect up to 4 prompts on the left with their matching answers on the right. The right-hand column is automatically shuffled so participants can't copy the order. Both text and images are supported on either side. Partial scoring is available — participants earn a proportional share of points for each correctly matched pair, even if they don't get them all right.

**When to use it:** Vocabulary and definition matching, country-capital or term-concept quizzes, connecting historical figures to events, or any knowledge check where the relationship between two items is what you're testing.

---

## Correct Order {#correct-order}

The Correct Order slide is a scored quiz type where you enter up to 7 statements in the right order and AhaSlides shuffles them for participants. Players drag and drop the statements into the sequence they believe is correct and submit before the timer runs out. There is no partial scoring — participants must get every statement in the right position to earn any points.

**When to use it:** Chronological timelines, step-by-step process sequences, ranked priority lists, or any question where the correct answer is an order rather than a selection.

---

## Type Answer {#type-answer}

The Type Answer slide (also called the Short Answer slide) is a scored quiz type where participants type their answer in a free-text box with no multiple-choice options available. You define a **correct answer to display** and any number of **other accepted answers** — including common misspellings and abbreviations. An answer scores points if it matches any accepted answer exactly. After the question, you can manually accept additional answers on the fly for responses that are correct but weren't in your list. Because there's nothing to guess from, Type Answer is a much truer test of knowledge than multiple choice.

**When to use it:** Vocabulary recall, capital cities, formulas, dates, definitions — any topic where you want to test whether participants can produce the answer from memory, not just recognise it.

---

## Pick Answer {#pick-answer}

The Pick Answer slide is the classic scored multiple-choice quiz question. Participants choose from up to 8 answer options on their device, score points for selecting the correct one(s), and climb the leaderboard. You can mark more than one option as correct, which activates a partial scoring option. Hints (up to 3) are also available — only you reveal them during the live question. Pick Answer is the most versatile quiz slide and the best starting point for any scored quiz.

**When to use it:** Any knowledge check where you want to provide answer options — classroom reviews, trivia, training comprehension checks, or icebreaker games.

## Pin on Image

The Pin on Image slide invites participants to tap or click directly on a custom image to place a pin as their response. As pins arrive, your presenter screen builds a live heatmap of where your audience is focusing. There are no pre-set answer zones — pins cluster naturally wherever participants tap. You control how many pins each participant can place (default is 1) and can set a time limit or hide results until you're ready to reveal. Upload any image: a map, diagram, product screenshot, floor plan, or photo.

**When to use it:** Location spotting on maps, UX and design feedback on screenshots, anatomy or equipment identification on diagrams, or team retrospectives using a 2×2 matrix image.

## Idea Board

The Idea Board slide collects text (and optionally image) submissions from your audience and organises them into themes. You can define **predefined groups** before participants submit (best when the structure is already known) or use **AI grouping** to let AhaSlides automatically cluster submissions into themes after they come in. You then refine the groupings by dragging cards between groups, renaming groups, and creating new ones. Optionally, add a voting stage where participants upvote the ideas they find most valuable — most-voted ideas rise to the top of the results.

**When to use it:** Sprint retrospectives (what went well / what didn't), brainstorming and design sprints, requirements gathering, customer or employee feedback collection, or any session where you need to move from raw ideas to organised, prioritised themes.

---

## Diagram

The Diagram slide lets you build and display structured visuals directly in AhaSlides — no external tool or image upload required. You enter labels, values, or node connections in the right panel and the diagram renders live on the canvas. There are seven diagram types: **Relation** (flowcharts and network diagrams), **Hierarchy** (org charts and decision trees), **Items** (step sequences, timelines, funnels, pyramids), **Chart** (bar, column, line, pie, donut), **Word Cloud** (weighted static word cloud), **Compare** (SWOT, quadrants, binary comparisons), and **Advanced** (raw AntV infographic syntax for full control). An optional streaming animation can reveal the diagram progressively when the slide appears.

**When to use it:** Presenting processes, structures, data, or relationships where a static image would be too rigid. The Diagram slide is fully editable in the editor — update labels and values without re-uploading anything.

## Content

The Content slide is a non-interactive, freeform canvas slide for displaying text, images, GIFs, emoji, and shapes. You edit directly on the canvas by clicking and dragging blocks into place. Each block can be moved, rotated, layered, and animated independently. Participants see the slide on their devices but can't interact with it (they can still send reactions). Use the five icons at the top of the slide to add new text blocks, images, GIFs, emoji, or shapes.

**When to use it:** Title slides, section dividers, instructions, image-heavy content, video embeds, or any slide where you need a designed layout rather than a structured interactive format.

<Note>
  The Content slide requires a minimum screen width of **1,180 px** to edit. If your screen is narrower, zoom out in your browser to access the editor.
</Note>
