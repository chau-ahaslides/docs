# Using the Pin on Image Slide

The Pin on Image slide invites participants to tap or click directly on a custom image to place a pin — their answer is a position, not a word or number. Use it to mark locations on a map, identify a part of a diagram, heatmap a product screenshot, or highlight anything that benefits from a spatial response. Results show all pins clustered live on the presenter screen.

## How the Pin on Image slide works

When you present a Pin on Image slide, participants see your chosen image on their device screens. They tap or click anywhere on the image to drop a pin — that position is their response. Once pins start coming in, your presenter screen shows all of them overlaid on the image in real time, building up a live heatmap of where your audience is focusing.

There are no pre-set answer zones, which means you can use any image and let responses cluster naturally. Each participant can place one or more pins depending on your setting.

## Setting up your Pin on Image slide

### 1. Add the slide

In the editor, click **New slide** (top-left) and select **Pin on Image** from the slide type picker. You'll find it in the **Unscored** section.

### 2. Write your question

Click into the **Your question** field in the Content panel and type the prompt you want participants to respond to. Be specific about where you want them to pin — for example: "Where would you put the primary CTA on this page?" or "Click on the region you think had the highest sales."

### 3. Add your image

Click the **Add image** area in the Content panel to open the image library. You have four options:

- **Upload** — drag in a file or browse from your device (JPEG, PNG, GIF, SVG — up to 15 MB).
- **My Images** — pick from files you've already uploaded to AhaSlides.
- **Stock Photos** — search thousands of free Unsplash photos and select one directly — no upload needed.
- **GIFs & Stickers** — add an animated image for lighter activities.

After selecting or uploading, the crop tool opens so you can frame the image exactly as you want it before saving.

> **Tip:** Diagrams, maps, product mockups, photos, and floor plans all work well. The image fills the participant screen, so landscape-orientation images tend to read better than portrait.

### 4. Set pins per participant

The **Pins per participant** field controls how many pins each participant can drop. The default is **1**. Increase it if you want participants to mark multiple things on the image — for example, circling both the most and least important elements on a slide.

### 5. Adjust optional settings

Scroll down in the Content panel to configure:

- **Time limit** — Toggle this on to add a countdown timer. Once it runs out, responses are automatically locked.
- **Close submission** — Toggle this on to manually lock submissions while presenting — useful when you want to move on before the timer expires.
- **Hide results** — Check this to keep pins hidden from participants during the live session. Useful for reveal-style activities where you want to discuss where people pinned before showing the full heatmap.

## Presenting the Pin on Image slide

Click **Present** in the top header to go live. Participants join at the access code shown on screen. Once they land on the slide, they'll see your image and a prompt to drop their pin. As responses come in, you'll see pins appear live on your presenter view — the image builds up as a heatmap in real time.

## Audience experience

On the participant's screen, the image fills most of the display. They tap or click once (or more, if you increased pins per participant) to place a pin at their chosen spot. Participants can reposition their pin before submitting — dragging it to a new spot updates the response instantly.

## Use cases

- **Geography and maps** — Ask participants to pin their home country, a region on a political map, or a location they visited.
- **Product and UX feedback** — Show a website screenshot and ask where they'd expect to find the pricing button.
- **Training and assessment** — Display an anatomy diagram or equipment schematic and ask participants to identify a specific part.
- **Team retrospectives** — Load a 2×2 matrix image and ask everyone to pin where the last sprint landed.
